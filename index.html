What This Is

NOT FOR
EVERYONE

Most guys are spinning their wheels, training consistently for months, even years, and looking exactly the same. Not because they lack effort. Because they lack structure. Random workouts and random progression produce random results. Monk Mode is not random.

This is a 10-week, 6-day Push/Pull/Legs program built around one non-negotiable principle: progressive overload on the compounds that matter. Squat and bench increase every single week for three weeks, then you deload and hit it again heavier in Wave 2. Every muscle group is trained twice a week — Push A and Push B, Pull A and Pull B, Legs A and Legs B — giving you the training frequency to drive real adaptation without burning out. Sunday is accessory: mobility, core, and shoulder longevity work that keeps you training hard for years, not just 10 weeks.

Face pulls appear on every pull day without exception. Hip mobility is built into every leg session before the first work set. These are not optional extras. They are the reason you will walk out of this program healthier than you walked in. Most strength programs ignore shoulder health until something breaks. This one addresses it from day one.

The structure is deliberate. The execution is on you. Two loading waves, each building from baseline through overload, separated by a deload that makes the next wave possible. Ten weeks from now you will squat heavier, press heavier, and look completely different. Most men never commit long enough to find out what they're capable of. You will.

🏋️
Strength First
Back squat and bench press add load every single week of each wave. Not when you feel like it, every week. Progressive overload on the primary compounds is how your body changes. Everything else is secondary.
🔁
Twice a Week
Each muscle group trained twice per week, the minimum effective frequency for maximizing both strength and hypertrophy. Push A and Push B hit chest and shoulders differently. Pull A and Pull B cover different angles of the back. Legs A is squat-dominant; Legs B hits the posterior chain. Frequency is what separates serious programs from gym noise.
🦵
Move Like an Athlete
Hip 90/90 switches, couch stretch, and goblet squat holds are programmed before every leg day, not as an afterthought, but as direct prep for squat depth and posterior chain loading. Tight hips don't just limit your squat. They shift load to your lower back. Fix the hips; fix the lift.
💪
Built to Last
Face pulls, band pull-aparts, and shoulder CARs appear on every push and pull day. External rotator health is the reason heavy bench pressers blow out their shoulders, and the reason this program's pressing volume is sustainable for the full 10 weeks and beyond.

The pump you're feeling at this point in the session is maximizing cellular swelling stimulus. Don't rush through it.

Hip Flexor Stretch (couch stretch)
3 sets × 60 sec/side
30 sec
~7 min
▼
The single most important mobility exercise for anyone who squats and sits. Tight psoas and rectus femoris cause anterior pelvic tilt, which limits squat depth, increases lower back stress, and flattens the glute response. Rear foot on a bench or couch, front shin vertical, drive your hip toward the floor. You should feel this deep in the front of the rear hip. Hold the tension — don't wiggle around. 60 seconds is the minimum effective dose for actual tissue change.
Hip 90/90 Switches
3 sets × 10/side
30 sec
~5 min
▼
Sit with both knees bent at 90° — one in front, one to the side. Rotate your pelvis to switch sides, moving through internal and external rotation of the hip joint. This is the most direct way to train hip mobility in the pattern that affects squatting and sprinting. Don't use your hands to push your knee down. Let the range come from actual hip rotation. Over 10 weeks of Sunday sessions this will measurably change your squat depth.
Pigeon Pose
3 sets × 60 sec/side
30 sec
~7 min
▼
Targets the piriformis and external hip rotators — the muscles responsible for hip tightness that presents as knee cave during squats. Front shin as close to horizontal as your current mobility allows. Don't let your pelvis rotate — keep both hips square to the floor. Breathe into the tension. 60 seconds minimum per side. If you're rushing through this, you're not getting the adaptation. It's supposed to be uncomfortable. That discomfort is the work.

Four sets of 20 on your rest day keeps the shoulder infrastructure healthy between heavy push and pull sessions.

Light load — the goal is rotator cuff activation, not strength stimulus. Dead Bug
3 sets × 10/side
45 sec
~6 min
▼
Lie on your back, arms straight to the ceiling, hips and knees at 90°. Lower the opposite arm and leg simultaneously toward the floor — without letting your lower back come off the ground. That's the whole exercise. The lower back must stay in contact with the floor throughout. This trains anti-extension core stability — the ability to resist lumbar extension under load. That's the same stability you need to brace properly under a squat bar. Don't rush the reps.
Pallof Press
3 sets × 12/side
45 sec
~6 min
▼
Stand sideways to a cable stack at chest height. Hold the handle at your sternum, brace hard, and press straight out in front of you — the cable will try to rotate you toward it. Resist that rotation completely. This trains anti-rotation core strength, which is the stability pattern used in almost every athletic movement and heavy compound lift. You should feel this in your obliques and deep core. Pause at full extension for 1 second.
Ab Wheel Rollout
3 sets × 10–12 reps
60 sec
~6 min
▼
One of the highest difficulty core exercises — don't let the simple appearance fool you. Roll out to a position where your body is parallel or near-parallel to the floor, maintaining a completely neutral spine. The moment your lower back arches, you've gone too far. Start from your knees until you can control the full range. This builds the same bracing pattern you use in heavy squats and deadlifts — core rigidity under axial load.
Single Leg Glute Bridge
3 sets × 15/side
45 sec
~6 min
▼
Lie on your back, one foot flat on the floor, the other leg extended. Drive through the heel and lift your hips until your body forms a straight line from shoulder to knee. Squeeze the glute hard at the top — most people fire their hamstring and back instead. The glute should be doing the work. Single-leg variation addresses bilateral glute strength imbalances that develop from asymmetrical squat patterns, and directly improves hip stability for squatting and sprinting.
T-Spine Rotation (foam roller)
3 sets × 10/side
30 sec
~4 min
▼
Place the foam roller perpendicular to your spine at mid-back level (thoracic spine, not lower back). Support your head, cross your arms, and rotate to each side. Thoracic mobility is critical for overhead pressing mechanics — a stiff t-spine forces the shoulder to compensate, which is where impingement patterns develop. Also directly affects your ability to keep an upright torso in the front squat. 10 reps each side, letting the weight of your arms rotate you further each rep.
Jefferson Curl (very light)
2 sets × 8 reps
60 sec
~4 min
▼
Stand on a box or step, hold 10–25 lbs, and slowly curl your spine down vertebra by vertebra — chin to chest first, then thoracic, then lumbar. This is controlled spinal flexion under minimal load, which builds tensile strength in the spinal erectors and improves flexion range of motion. Never heavy. Never rushed. This is the opposite of every other movement in this program — the spine is rounding deliberately. That deliberate control is what makes it safe and beneficial.
