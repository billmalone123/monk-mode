What This Is

NOT FOR
EVERYONE

Most guys are spinning their wheels, training consistently for months, even years, and looking exactly the same. Not because they lack effort. Because they lack structure. Random workouts and random progression produce random results. Monk Mode is not random.

This is a 10-week Arnold Split built around one non-negotiable principle: progressive overload on the compounds that matter. Bench and barbell squat increase every single week for three weeks, then you deload and hit it again heavier in Wave 2. The pattern is 3 days on, 1 off — Chest + Back, Shoulders + Arms, Legs + Abs — then rest, then repeat with B-day variations. Each muscle group gets hit twice a week from different angles. Day 4 is rest and stretch: mobility, shoulder maintenance, and recovery work that keeps you training hard for years, not just 10 weeks.

Face pulls appear on every pull day without exception. Hip mobility is built into every leg session before the first work set. These are not optional extras. They are the reason you will walk out of this program healthier than you walked in. Most strength programs ignore shoulder health until something breaks. This one addresses it from day one.

The structure is deliberate. The execution is on you. Two loading waves, each building from baseline through overload, separated by a deload that makes the next wave possible. Ten weeks from now you will squat heavier, press heavier, and look completely different. Most men never commit long enough to find out what they're capable of. You will.

Strength First
Bench press, barbell squat, back squat, and OHP add load every single week of each wave. Not when you feel like it, every week. Progressive overload on the primary compounds is how your body changes. Everything else is secondary.
Twice a Week
Each muscle group trained twice per week on the Arnold Split rotation — 3 on, 1 off. Day A exercises and Day B exercises hit the same muscles from different angles so you never adapt and stagnate. Chest + Back A is flat press machine focus. Chest + Back B is incline and cable focus. Frequency is what separates serious programs from gym noise.
Move Like an Athlete
Hip 90/90 switches, couch stretch, and goblet squat holds are programmed before every leg day, not as an afterthought, but as direct prep for squat depth and posterior chain loading. Tight hips don't just limit your squat. They shift load to your lower back. Fix the hips; fix the lift.
Built to Last
Face pulls, band pull-aparts, and shoulder CARs appear on every push and pull day. External rotator health is the reason heavy bench pressers blow out their shoulders, and the reason this program's pressing volume is sustainable for the full 10 weeks and beyond.

Hip Flexor Stretch (couch stretch)
2 sets × 60 sec/side
30 sec
~7 min
▼
The single most important mobility exercise for anyone who trains legs. Tight psoas and rectus femoris limit squat depth, increase lower back stress, and flatten the glute response. Rear foot on a bench or couch, front shin vertical, drive your hip toward the floor. Hold the tension — don't wiggle around. 60 seconds is the minimum effective dose for actual tissue change.
Hip 90/90 Switches
2 sets × 10/side
30 sec
~5 min
▼
Sit with both knees bent at 90° — one in front, one to the side. Rotate your pelvis to switch sides, moving through internal and external rotation of the hip joint. Don't use your hands to push your knee down. Let the range come from actual hip rotation. Consistent sessions of this will measurably change your squat depth over time.
Pigeon Pose
2 sets × 60 sec/side
30 sec
~7 min
▼
Targets the piriformis and external hip rotators — the muscles responsible for hip tightness that presents as knee cave during squats. Front shin as close to horizontal as your current mobility allows. Keep both hips square to the floor. Breathe into the tension. 60 seconds minimum per side. It's supposed to be uncomfortable. That discomfort is the work.
T-Spine Rotation (foam roller)
2 sets × 10/side
30 sec
~4 min
▼
Place the foam roller perpendicular to your spine at mid-back level. Support your head, cross your arms, and rotate to each side. Thoracic mobility is critical for overhead pressing mechanics — a stiff t-spine forces the shoulder to compensate. 10 reps each side, letting the weight of your arms rotate you further each rep.
Band Pull-Aparts
2 sets × 20 reps
30 sec
~5 min
▼
Hold the band at shoulder width, arms straight, and pull it apart to chest level — ending in a "T" position. Pinch shoulder blades together hard at end range. This trains the posterior deltoid and external rotators, directly counterbalancing the internal rotation load of pressing. The shoulder maintenance you do on rest day is the reason this shoulder will still train heavy at 40.
Face Pull with External Rotation
2 sets × 20 reps
45 sec
~5 min
▼
Light load — rotator cuff activation, not strength stimulus. Pull to your face with high elbows, then add external rotation at the end: press handles up and outward so your hands end above your elbows. This end-range external rotation is what makes this version more effective than a standard face pull. Think of this as the direct antidote to every pressing session you've done this week.
Jefferson Curl (very light)
2 sets × 8 reps
60 sec
~4 min
▼
Stand on a box or step, hold 10–25 lbs, and slowly curl your spine down vertebra by vertebra — chin to chest first, then thoracic, then lumbar. Controlled spinal flexion under minimal load builds tensile strength in the spinal erectors. Never heavy. Never rushed. The spine is rounding deliberately — that deliberate control is what makes it safe and beneficial.
